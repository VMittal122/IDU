Criterion A — Evaluating 8 marks
Compare and contrast how Science and Individuals & Societies each contribute to understanding the relationship between genetics and identity.
Sources: Refs 1, 3, 4 · Nature/nurture, twin studies, separated twins
+
Context

The question of whether our identity is determined by our genes (nature) or our environment (nurture) has been examined through both scientific research and sociological theory. References 1, 3, and 4 draw on both Science and Individuals & Societies to explore this question.

Task

With reference to at least two of References 1, 3, and 4, compare and contrast how Science and Individuals & Societies each contribute to understanding the relationship between genetics and identity. In your answer, you must:

  • Explain two ways in which Science contributes to understanding this relationship
  • Explain two ways in which Individuals & Societies contributes to understanding this relationship
  • Explain one limitation of relying on Science alone to understand identity
  • Explain one limitation of relying on I&S alone to understand identity
  • In your conclusion, evaluate which discipline contributes more completely to understanding identity, and justify your answer
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
0 0 No achievement at any level
1–2 1–2 Describes how one or both disciplines relate to identity in a general way. Does not use sources as evidence. No comparison made.
3–4 3–4 Explains how each discipline contributes to understanding identity with reference to at least one source. Some comparison attempted but not developed. Limitations may be mentioned without explanation.
5–6 5–6 Clearly explains two contributions from each discipline, citing specific evidence from sources (e.g. 0.92 MZ height correlation from Ref 3; socialisation concept from Ref 1). Limitations explained. Conclusion present but evaluation not fully justified.
7–8 7–8 Thorough analysis of how both disciplines contribute to, and are limited in, understanding identity. Uses specific source evidence (data values, case examples, concepts). Clear evaluation in conclusion with a justified position on which discipline contributes more completely, acknowledging complexity. Shows genuine interdisciplinary insight.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Science contributions: (1) Ref 3 quantifies genetic contribution — 0.92 MZ vs 0.47 DZ for height proves a strong heritable component to physical identity. (2) Ref 4's separated twins (Divia/Lilly) show behavioural traits (nail-biting, leg-crossing) persist across radically different environments, confirming genetic behavioural basis.

I&S contributions: (1) Ref 1 introduces socialisation — the process by which cultural environment, peer groups, and family shape identity beyond biology. (2) Ref 4's music difference (rock vs classical) shows cultural environment diverges outcomes even with identical genetics.

Conclusion: Neither discipline alone is sufficient — Science without I&S cannot explain why twins with identical genes diverge in music preference; I&S without Science cannot explain why they converge on the same career, habits, and posture across different cultures. A strong answer notes that heritability averages 49% (Ref 3) — literally suggesting equal contributions — supporting the conclusion that both disciplines are necessary and complementary.

Criterion A — Evaluating 8 marks
To what extent does reference to both Science and Individuals & Societies provide a more complete understanding of ultra-processed food's impact on identity than either discipline alone?
Sources: Refs 5, 6 · UPF health data, twin experiment
+
Task

With reference to References 5 and 6, evaluate the extent to which combining Science and Individuals & Societies produces a more complete understanding of how diet shapes identity. In your answer, you must:

  • Explain what Science alone reveals about UPF's effect on the body (use data from Ref 5 and/or Ref 6)
  • Explain what I&S alone reveals about the social factors shaping dietary identity
  • Explain what insight can ONLY be achieved by combining both disciplines
  • Evaluate one significant limitation of the evidence used in your argument
  • In conclusion, state and justify the extent to which interdisciplinary analysis adds value beyond single-discipline analysis
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 General description of what UPF does. No source data cited. No discipline analysis.
3–4 3–4 Explains Science or I&S contribution but not both. Some source reference. Conclusion not evaluative.
5–6 5–6 Both contributions explained with source evidence. One limitation noted. Conclusion present. Does not fully articulate what combining the disciplines adds.
7–8 7–8 Clear explanation of both disciplinary contributions with specific data (e.g. 52% obesity increase in high-UPF group; Aimee's blood marker changes in 14 days). Articulates an insight that neither discipline produces alone. Limitation evaluated (e.g. cross-sectional design or N=1 pair). Conclusion evaluates the extent with a justified position.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Science alone: Ref 5 shows dose-response — obesity rate ~21% (low UPF) vs ~32% (high UPF). Ref 6 shows blood sugar and fat markers worsen in 14 days. Science explains the biological mechanism: satiety hormone disruption, rapid metabolic change.

I&S alone: Explains why people eat UPF — food insecurity, marketing of UPF as 'healthy,' peer social environments, convenience culture. Aimee's case shows food identity can be unconsciously constructed by social marketing.

Combined insight: Only interdisciplinary analysis reveals that diet inequality is simultaneously a biological health issue AND a structural social justice issue. Poor communities have both higher UPF consumption (I&S: food insecurity) AND worse health outcomes (Science: dose-response). Neither discipline alone captures this loop.

Limitation: Ref 6 is N=1 pair — Newsweek, not peer-reviewed. Ref 5 is cross-sectional — cannot prove causation. A sophisticated answer acknowledges this while arguing the twin design still provides strong causal evidence despite the small sample.

Criterion A — Evaluating 8 marks
Compare and contrast how Science (somatotyping) and Individuals & Societies (social access) explain the different athletic prospects of Akira Luwi and Cirrus Rodriguez.
Sources: Refs 7, 8 · Somatotyping, athlete profiles
+
Task

With reference to References 7 and 8, compare and contrast how Science and Individuals & Societies each contribute to explaining the different athletic prospects of Akira Luwi and Cirrus Rodriguez. In your answer, you must:

  • Explain two similarities between Akira and Cirrus from a scientific perspective (somatotype/genetics)
  • Explain two differences from an Individuals & Societies perspective (social structures, access, resources)
  • Provide specific details from the profiles to support each point
  • In conclusion, evaluate whether Science or I&S better explains the gap in their athletic prospects, and justify your answer
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 General comparison without using somatotype data or specific profile details.
3–4 3–4 Uses some profile data. One or two points from each discipline. Conclusion descriptive rather than evaluative.
5–6 5–6 Clear use of somatotype numbers and access data. Both disciplines addressed. Conclusion offers a position but justification underdeveloped.
7–8 7–8 Specific data throughout (3-7-2 vs 5-5-1 somatotypes; 1km vs 50km; affluent vs unemployed). Evaluative conclusion with clear justification: I&S better explains the gap because both athletes have appropriate genetic potential — what differs is entirely social/environmental.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Similarities (Science): Both Akira (3-7-2) and Cirrus (5-5-1) have dominant mesomorph components suited to power sports. Both are young males (19 and 20) in peak physical developmental years. Scientifically, both have appropriate genetic potential for elite sport.

Differences (I&S): (1) Access — Akira's facilities are 1km away; Cirrus's are 50km. (2) Socioeconomic status — Akira comes from an affluent family; Cirrus is unemployed following a failed professional contract. Additionally Cirrus has a knee injury — a physical setback with social consequences (no income for rehabilitation).

Conclusion: I&S better explains the gap because scientifically both athletes have appropriate potential. The entire difference in prospects is social and environmental — income, geography, injury support structures. This supports the IDU core argument that nature provides potential; nurture determines realisation.

Criterion B — Synthesizing 8 marks
As a sports development officer, design an athlete support programme for Cirrus Rodriguez that integrates scientific and social solutions to maximise his athletic potential.
Sources: Refs 5, 6, 7, 8 · Somatotyping, diet, athlete access
+
Scenario

You are a sports development officer working with the National Sports Council of South America. Cirrus Rodriguez (Ref 8) has been identified as a genetically promising athlete (somatotype 5-5-1) whose development has been blocked by socioeconomic and geographic barriers. Your brief is to design a support programme that addresses both the scientific dimensions (body type optimisation, nutrition) and the social dimensions (access, financial support) of his development.

Task

Using References 5, 6, 7, and 8, design an athlete support programme for Cirrus Rodriguez. Your programme must justify:

  • Scientific recommendations: how Cirrus's somatotype (Ref 7) informs the specific sport and training approach, and how dietary guidance (Refs 5/6) should address body composition
  • Social recommendations: how to address the geographic (50km), financial (unemployed), and physical (knee injury) barriers using I&S concepts (access, social mobility, intersectionality)
  • How the integration of Science and I&S produces a more effective programme than either discipline alone could design
  • One potential challenge to implementing this programme, from both a scientific and social perspective
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 General suggestions without reference to sources. No integration of disciplines.
3–4 3–4 Addresses either Science or I&S but not both. Some source reference. Programme lacks specificity.
5–6 5–6 Both disciplines addressed with specific source references. Integration attempted. Challenges mentioned without depth.
7–8 7–8 Specific scientific recommendations (somatotype match, dietary guidance using NOVA/Ref 6 findings). Specific social recommendations (transport subsidy, rehabilitation access, income support). Clear articulation of how integration produces better outcomes. Challenge analysed from both disciplinary perspectives.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Science: Cirrus's 5-5-1 somatotype (endo-mesomorph) suggests football or rugby as appropriate sports. Training programme should target maintaining mesomorphic strength while reducing endomorphic excess through aerobic conditioning. Dietary guidance: reduce UPF (Ref 5 shows dose-response with obesity risk) — crucial because excess endomorphy reduces athletic efficiency. Ref 6 shows even 14 days of dietary change produces measurable metabolic shifts — short-term intervention is viable.

I&S: Relocate Cirrus to a sports residential facility near training centres (addresses 50km barrier). Provide a sports scholarship equivalent to Victoria's (Ref 8) — institutional support transforms access. Knee rehabilitation funded publicly — using social mobility through sport as economic justification for investment.

Integration insight: Science alone produces a training programme Cirrus cannot access. I&S alone provides access without optimising the training. Combined: access to facilities + somatotype-appropriate training + dietary intervention = the full programme.

Challenge: Scientific: knee rehabilitation timelines are uncertain — training load must be managed carefully. Social: public sports funding in South America may be limited — political will required.

Criterion B — Synthesizing 8 marks
Create a public health campaign targeting adolescent UPF consumption that integrates scientific evidence and sociological understanding of peer identity formation.
Sources: Refs 5, 6 · UPF health data, twin experiment, adolescent peak
+
Scenario

Health Canada has commissioned a campaign to reduce ultra-processed food (UPF) consumption among adolescents aged 13–18, following the data in Reference 5 which shows this age group consumes over 50% of daily calories from UPF — the highest of any age group. You are leading the campaign design team.

Task

Using References 5 and 6, design and justify a public health campaign targeting adolescent UPF consumption. Your campaign must integrate:

  • Scientific evidence: specific data from Ref 5 and/or Ref 6 that justifies the urgency of the campaign
  • Social insight: an I&S explanation of WHY adolescents consume so much UPF (peer identity, socialisation, marketing) — and how your campaign addresses this social mechanism
  • A specific campaign element that could only have been designed by combining both disciplines
  • Justify how mathematical precision (using the data) and social communication (targeting peer identity) together make the campaign more effective
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 Generic campaign without source evidence or discipline integration.
3–4 3–4 Uses either the data OR the social insight but not both. Campaign plausible but not interdisciplinary.
5–6 5–6 Both disciplines used. Specific data cited. Social mechanism identified. Campaign element described.
7–8 7–8 Specific data used (52% UPF, 14-day blood marker changes from Ref 6). Social mechanism explained (peer identity, secondary socialisation). Campaign element that only works through integration (e.g. using peers as ambassadors — social trust + biological evidence). Clear articulation of why integration matters.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Scientific evidence: Ref 5 shows children 6–12 and adolescents 13–18 consume over 50% of daily calories from UPF — significantly above adults. Ref 6 shows blood markers worsened in just 14 days on a UPF diet in a young, healthy person (Aimee's shock finding). These data points provide specific, compelling urgency.

Social insight: Adolescent UPF spike is not genetic — it maps onto secondary socialisation (peer groups). Eating patterns become identity signals: UPF brands are marketed as social belonging markers. The campaign must work WITH social identity formation, not against it.

Integrated element: A peer-ambassador programme where popular adolescents share their own Aimee Kingston-style 14-day dietary experiment on social media. The Science provides credibility (real biomarkers, real data). The I&S provides the channel (peer influence, identity re-framing: healthy eating as the new social identity). Neither discipline alone designs this — Science without social insight produces a pamphlet no one reads; I&S without Science lacks the biological urgency that makes the message compelling.

Criterion B — Synthesizing 8 marks
Write a briefing document for a school's pastoral team explaining how a student's name, body type, and socioeconomic background interact to shape their academic and sporting identity — and what the school can do about it.
Sources: Refs 2, 3, 7, 8 · Nominative determinism, heritability, somatotyping, athlete profiles
+
Task

Using References 2, 3, 7, and 8, write a briefing document for school pastoral staff. Your document must synthesize Science and I&S to explain:

  • What Science tells us about the genetic basis of physical and cognitive traits that shape student identity (Refs 3 and 7)
  • What I&S tells us about how names, social labels, and access structures shape how students see themselves and are treated (Refs 2 and 8)
  • Two specific, evidence-based recommendations for the school that draw on BOTH disciplines
  • How combining Science and I&S allows the school to support students more effectively than a single-discipline approach
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 General advice without source evidence. No discipline distinction.
3–4 3–4 One discipline used well, the other superficially. Recommendations present but not evidence-based.
5–6 5–6 Both disciplines addressed with source evidence. Recommendations clear and linked to sources. Integration attempted.
7–8 7–8 Specific evidence throughout (heritability values, somatotype numbers, name meaning examples). Recommendations are genuinely interdisciplinary — not just one from each discipline. Integration articulated: explains why both lenses together produce better pastoral support than either alone.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Science contribution: Ref 3 shows higher cognitive function has 0.69 MZ heritability — students inherit cognitive tendencies. Ref 7: body type is largely heritable (0.87–0.92) — physical identity in sport has a genetic foundation teachers should understand rather than punish or ignore.

I&S contribution: Ref 2's labelling theory and self-fulfilling prophecy: teacher labels ('gifted,' 'struggling') function like names — they shape student self-concept. Ref 8: Akira's affluent background vs Cirrus's unemployment mirrors socioeconomic inequality in schools — some students' potential is blocked by structural factors, not individual failure.

Recommendations: (1) Sport matching based on somatotype assessment (Science) + structured access support for economically disadvantaged students (I&S). (2) Conscious positive labelling practices that acknowledge genetic tendencies without biological determinism — recognise a student's genetic starting point while providing social scaffolding to help them exceed it.

Criterion C — Reflecting 8 marks
Using the scientific lens and the sociological lens, discuss how examining identity through both perspectives changes or deepens your understanding of whether identity is 'given' or 'chosen.'
Sources: All references · Core IDU question
+
Task

The central question of this IDU is whether identity is determined for us (by genetics, names, body type) or constructed by us (through choices, relationships, culture). Using the scientific lens and the sociological lens as informed by the sources, discuss how examining this question through both perspectives shapes your understanding. In your answer, you must:

  • Present an argument from the scientific perspective on whether identity is given or chosen (with source evidence)
  • Present an argument from the sociological perspective on whether identity is given or chosen (with source evidence)
  • Present a third argument that can only emerge from combining both perspectives
  • Use specific details from at least three sources to support your points
  • Reflect on how examining this question through both lenses has changed or deepened your thinking
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 Personal opinion only. No source use. No lens analysis.
3–4 3–4 One or two perspectives presented with limited source evidence. Does not articulate what combining perspectives adds.
5–6 5–6 Both perspectives presented with source evidence. Third combined argument attempted. Reflection is superficial.
7–8 7–8 Three distinct arguments, each citing specific evidence. Combined argument clearly articulates an insight that neither perspective produces alone. Reflection is genuinely analytical — identifies a specific way thinking has changed, not just 'I learned a lot.'
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Scientific perspective (given): Ref 3's average heritability of 49% — and up to 0.92 for height — shows core identity traits are biologically given. Ref 4's separated twins (Fred/Callum, same headache, both married Jane) suggest even relationship patterns may have heritable roots. From a scientific lens, a substantial part of identity is given before any choice is made.

Sociological perspective (given AND chosen, but socially): Ref 2 shows names are socially given — assigned by cultural practices. Ref 8 shows that social access (Victoria's scholarship vs Cirrus's unemployment) gives or denies opportunities — identity is constrained by structures outside individual control. I&S shows identity is given not just biologically but socially — the society you're born into assigns labels and life chances.

Combined insight: Combining Science and I&S reveals that identity is neither simply given nor simply chosen — it is a co-production. Ref 6 is the clearest example: Aimee and Nancy have identical genetics (given), but Aimee's post-study decision to eat more mindfully (chosen) — enabled by the knowledge she gained (social/educational experience) — changed her health identity in 14 days. The biological and the social are inseparable in constructing who we are.

Reflection: Before this IDU, I assumed identity was mostly about personal choice. Science showed me how much is genetically pre-set. But I&S showed me that the social environment — income, name, access — also 'sets' identity beyond individual control. The deepest insight is that even what seems like a 'choice' (what we eat, what sport we play) is heavily structured by forces neither genetic nor volitional.

Criterion C — Reflecting 8 marks
Using the scientific lens and the sociological lens, discuss how these two perspectives change or deepen our understanding of whether sporting success is meritocratic — achieved by talent and effort alone.
Sources: Refs 3, 7, 8 · Heritability, somatotyping, athlete profiles
+
Task

The common belief is that sport is a meritocracy — those with talent and who work hardest succeed. Using the scientific and sociological perspectives from References 3, 7, and 8, discuss how these two lenses reshape or deepen understanding of whether sporting success is truly meritocratic. In your answer, you must:

  • Present three arguments on how using these two lenses changes or deepens our understanding of sporting meritocracy
  • Use specific details from at least two sources to support your points
  • In your conclusion, reflect on how your understanding of sporting success has been changed by examining it through both lenses
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 States sport is or is not meritocratic without lens analysis or source evidence.
3–4 3–4 Uses one lens. Some source reference. Arguments lack development.
5–6 5–6 Both lenses used. Three arguments present but some lack specific evidence. Conclusion reflective but not deeply analytical.
7–8 7–8 Three distinct arguments each from or combining both lenses. Specific evidence (somatotype numbers, access data, heritability values). Conclusion genuinely reflects how both lenses together challenge meritocracy more thoroughly than either alone.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Argument 1 (Science lens): Somatotype is largely heritable (Ref 3: weight/height ~0.87–0.92 MZ). Sport favours certain body types (Ref 7). This means sporting advantage begins at conception — an athlete is partly 'born' to their sport, not purely earning it through effort. The meritocracy narrative ignores this biological pre-selection.

Argument 2 (I&S lens): Ref 8 shows Akira and Cirrus have comparable somatotypic potential but radically different social access. The meritocracy narrative ('work hard and you'll succeed') fails to account for geographic and financial barriers that prevent effort from translating into opportunity. The 50km barrier cannot be overcome by talent alone.

Argument 3 (Combined): Victoria Johnson (Ref 8) is the case that most challenges meritocracy — she has genetic advantage (1-6-2 somatotype), social advantage (scholarship), and even nominative advantage (Ref 2: name = 'winner'). If she succeeds, is it merit or accumulated advantage? Combining Science and I&S reveals that what looks like individual meritocratic achievement is often the result of compounding genetic and social privilege that pre-selects certain individuals for success before effort is even applied.

Reflection: Through Science alone, I would have concluded sport is partly unfair due to genetic luck. Through I&S alone, I would have focused on socioeconomic barriers. But combining them revealed a more disturbing insight: genetic and social advantages often coincide and compound (as in Victoria's case), making the illusion of meritocracy most powerful for those who need it least.

Criterion C — Reflecting 8 marks
Using the biological lens and the sociological lens, discuss how examining nominative determinism through both perspectives deepens understanding of the role of names in shaping identity.
Sources: Refs 2, 3, 4, 8 · Names, genetics, twins, athlete profiles
+
Task

Using the biological (scientific) and sociological lenses as evidenced in References 2, 3, 4, and 8, discuss how examining the role of names in shaping identity through both perspectives deepens our understanding. In your answer you must:

  • Present three arguments on how these two lenses change or deepen understanding of names and identity
  • Use specific details from the sources to support each argument
  • In your conclusion, reflect on what understanding the interaction between biological and social forces adds to our analysis of names and identity
▸ Show mark scheme & model guidance
Band Marks Descriptor
1–2 1–2 General description of nominative determinism. No lens analysis.
3–4 3–4 One or two arguments with source evidence. One lens dominant.
5–6 5–6 Three arguments present. Both lenses used. Evidence cited. Conclusion reflects on learning.
7–8 7–8 Three fully developed arguments. Biological lens challenges or complicates the sociological claim of nominative determinism. Combined insight emerges. Reflection genuinely analytical.
Model Answer Guidance (Band 7–8)

Argument 1 (Social lens — names shape identity): Ref 2's labelling theory: names assigned by parents create social expectations that shape self-concept. Victoria (winner) receives different social treatment from birth — teachers expect more, coaches invest more. Ref 8 shows Victoria has every advantage compounding — name, scholarship, access. The social mechanism of nominative determinism is plausible.

Argument 2 (Biological lens — challenges nominative determinism): Ref 3 shows 49% average heritability across traits. Ref 4's Baker twins share surname AND hobby — but both have genetics predisposing them to similar interests (gene-environment correlation). The biological lens suggests the Baker/baking coincidence may reflect genetic predisposition, not name influence. The sociological explanation may be a confound.

Argument 3 (Combined — most nuanced): The Winner/Loser paradox (Ref 2) is only fully explained by combining lenses. Social lens: the name 'Loser' triggered identity resistance (labelling theory + agency). Biological lens: Ref 3 shows cognitive function has 0.69 heritability — 'Loser' Lane may have been genetically predisposed to the intellectual determination required to become a detective despite the label. The interaction of genetic resilience and social label resistance produced his outcome — neither lens alone explains it.

Reflection: Names matter — but less because they change who we biologically are, and more because they shape how the social world treats us, and therefore what opportunities our biology gets to express.
