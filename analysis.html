Reference 01 · Video Script

Nature and Nurture

IB IDU introductory video — the theoretical framework for the whole unit
ScienceI&SGC ConnectionNot peer-reviewed

"Your genes might give you a starting point, but your environment helps shape how these traits develop."

— Reference 1, Nature and Nurture Video Script
🔬 Science Perspective
  • Nature = genotype: your complete DNA inherited from parents. Fixed at conception. Codes for biological traits — eye colour, potential height, metabolic tendencies.
  • Nurture = environment: every non-genetic influence from womb onwards — diet, schooling, relationships, cultural exposure, socioeconomic context.
  • Phenotype = genotype + environment: what is actually observable. Two identical genotypes (MZ twins) can produce different phenotypes in different environments — this is the scientific foundation of the whole unit.
  • Gene-Environment Interaction (GEI): the basketball analogy. Genetic height (nature) only becomes athletic height with training and coaching (nurture). The effect of a gene depends on the environment it operates in.
  • Epigenetics — not covered here but crucial: environment can chemically tag DNA to alter gene expression without changing the sequence.
🌍 Individuals & Societies Perspective
  • Socialisation: the lifelong process by which individuals learn societal norms, values, and behaviours. Primary (family) and secondary (peers, school, media) socialisation are both forms of "nurture".
  • Agency vs determinism: the nature/nurture debate is fundamentally about how much free choice we have. Are we biologically pre-programmed, or socially free agents?
  • Social construction of identity: the idea that identity is built through interactions and cultural practices — not biologically fixed. Culture, class, and community all shape who we become.
→ Global Context Connections
  • Your theoretical anchor — use to define nature/nurture in every essay before bringing evidence from other sources
  • Identity is co-produced and dynamic — not fixed at birth but reshaped throughout life by relationships
  • Relationships (coaches, parents, peers) are the nurture mechanism — directly connects identity to the GC theme of relationships
  • Every other source either tests the nature side, the nurture side, or their interaction — this source frames what they're testing
→ Ref 3 quantifies this→ Ref 4 illustrates this→ Ref 6 experiments on this
! Limitations to Critique
  • Extremely simplified — omits epigenetics, gene-environment correlations (rGE), and gene expression
  • Not peer-reviewed; designed for young learners — lacks scientific rigour
  • No data, statistics, or citations to support claims
  • Presents nature and nurture as equal partners — Ref 3 shows genetics often dominate
  • Basketball example conflates height potential with skill — two different types of genetic advantage
Reference 02 · Video Script

Nominative Determinism

Hypothesis that people gravitate toward life paths matching their name's meaning
I&SGC ConnectionAnecdotal
📋 Teacher's Official Inquiry Questions for This Source
Science Questions
  • Can a name directly affect biology, or does it influence behaviour through environment?
  • How could repeated labels affect a person's behaviour over time?
I&S Questions
  • Do labels and expectations shape identity?
  • Can society influence a person by attaching meaning to their name?
  • How might a name affect confidence, aspiration, or self-image?
Interdisciplinary Questions
  • Is identity partly shaped by what others expect from us?
  • Can a label become a self-fulfilling prophecy?
Core Concept Analysis

The Winner/Loser Paradox — Deep Interpretation

Brother named "Winner" → long criminal record. Brother named "Loser" → became a police detective. This directly falsifies the simple nominative determinism claim. From a Science lens, their identical environment (same family) should produce similar outcomes — it didn't. From an I&S lens: Labelling Theory (Becker) suggests "Loser" may have been driven to prove the label wrong — identity resistance. But the name still influenced the path, just not as expected. This ambiguity makes it the richest case study in the unit.

🌍 I&S Concepts Applied
  • Nominative determinism: hypothesis (not law) that name meaning shapes life path — weak scientific basis but sociologically interesting
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy (Merton, 1948): a belief that, by being held, causes itself to become true — "Victoria = winner" may generate winning behaviour through increased confidence and external validation
  • Labelling Theory (Becker): social labels shape self-concept and how others treat you — once "Loser" was labelled, it shaped every interaction
  • Cultural capital (Bourdieu): a prestigious name may carry subtle social advantages — people treat a "Victoria" as a winner from birth
  • Historical context: medieval surnames (Baker, Carpenter) were caused BY occupation — the modern claim reverses the causal arrow
🔬 Science Perspective
  • A name has no direct genetic effect — it does not alter DNA
  • But behavioural science shows: repeated social expectations shape neural pathways over time (neuroplasticity) — the environment of expectations becomes a real biological influence
  • This is the teacher's key science question: names don't change biology directly, but they change the social environment, which then changes biology through repeated experience
  • This is an indirect pathway from nurture (name label) → environmental response (expectations) → behaviour → potential neurological effect
→ Global Context Connections
  • A name is the first identity label — assigned before any personal choice is possible, by parents operating within cultural systems
  • Cross-reference Ref 8: Akira (brave warrior), Victoria (winner), Cirrus (cloud), Uju (plenty) — apply this framework to ask whether names predict athletic outcomes
  • Cross-reference Ref 4: Baker twins share surname AND both love baking — coincidence, genetics, or name influence?
  • The central GC tension: identity as assigned vs identity as chosen — names are the clearest case of assigned identity
→ Baker twins connection→ Athlete names
! Limitations
  • N=2 brothers — not statistically meaningful at all
  • Confirmation bias: we remember name-life matches and forget mismatches
  • No peer-reviewed studies cited; scientific evidence for nominative determinism is weak
  • Confounding variables: the brothers had different life events beyond just their names

📊 Trend Analysis

The ≈2× Rule: MZ correlations are consistently roughly double DZ correlations. This is the statistical fingerprint of heritability — if traits were purely environmental, MZ and DZ correlations would be equal. Physical traits (height 0.92, weight 0.87) have the strongest genetic signal. Cognitive function (0.69) shows genes also shape thinking and personality. No trait reaches 1.0 — environment always plays some role. This is the scientific proof that identity is never purely nature OR nurture.

🔬 Key Scientific Concepts
  • MZ twins: 100% shared DNA — any difference must be environmental
  • DZ twins: ~50% shared DNA (like siblings) — both share the same home, so the MZ-DZ gap isolates genetics
  • Heritability (h²): proportion of variation between people attributable to genetic differences. NOT "genes determine X% of you" — it's a population statistic about variation
  • Meta-analysis: combining 2,748 studies = massively higher statistical power than any single study
  • Additive genetic variation: 69% of traits explained by this model — shared family environment adds very little
→ GC Connections
  • Quantifies exactly how much identity is biologically given — the best scientific evidence for the nature side
  • Even cognitive function (how we think and decide) has 0.69 MZ heritability — our inner identity has a biological core
  • No trait = 1.0, so environment always contributes — identity is never purely genetic
  • Use specific numbers in essays: "0.92 MZ vs 0.47 DZ for height"
! Limitations
  • Equal Environments Assumption (EEA): assumes MZ and DZ are treated equally similarly — but MZ twins may be dressed the same, treated identically, inflating correlations
  • Correlation ≠ causation — no specific gene identified
  • Sample skewed toward Europe/N. America/Australia — limited global representation
Reference 04 · Web Blog

Twins Separated at Birth

Two case studies: Divia & Lilly (India/South Africa) · Fred & Callum
ScienceI&SGC ConnectionAnecdotal Blog
Case 1: Divia (India) & Lilly (South Africa)

Separated 34 Years — Converged Lives

Similarities: same hairstyle and hair habit, same shade of lipstick, same laugh, same degree (Anthropology), same career (museum), same hobby (baking), same nail-biting habit, same leg-crossing posture. Surname: Baker — both love baking (→ Ref 2 connection). Key difference: music taste (rock vs classical). This one difference is scientifically important — it shows cultural environment diverges outcomes even with identical genetics.

Case 2: Fred & Callum (both meaning: peace)

When Genetics Shapes Relationships

Same height, weight, brown curly hair. Both married a woman named Jane. Both divorced after exactly 12 years. Both love river fishing, both won fishing contests. Same unusual hammering headache. Both named dog Elvis. Both in law enforcement. The relationship patterns suggest heritable personality traits drive who we are attracted to and how we bond — connecting identity directly to the GC theme of relationships.

🔬 Science Perspective
  • Separated twin design = gold standard: no shared home environment means any similarity = genetic signal
  • Supports Ref 3 quantitatively: behavioural traits (habits, hobbies) AND physical traits show high MZ convergence even without shared environment
  • Gene-Environment Correlation (rGE): genetic predispositions may have actively led both to seek similar environments (Anthropology degrees, museums) independently — genes influence which environments we choose
  • Music difference = nurture override: cultural exposure (different radio, social circles, musical traditions) diverges preferences even with identical DNA
→ GC Connections
  • Core GC question: if two people raised in entirely different cultures end up nearly identical — what is individual identity?
  • Baker surname + baking hobby = direct Ref 2 essay link
  • Relationship patterns (married Jane, divorced at 12yr) suggests genetics shapes the bonds we form — identity connects to relationships at a biological level
  • Music difference = cultural identity matters even when biology is fixed
! Limitations
  • Only two cases — not statistically representative; likely selected for their dramatic similarities
  • Blog format — not peer-reviewed; may exaggerate similarities
  • Both pairs raised in middle-class families — hidden shared class variable could explain career/hobby similarities

📊 Chart 2 Interpretation — Dose-Response Pattern

Every health condition worsens consistently from low → medium → high UPF. Obesity shows the largest relative increase: 21% to 32% = +52% higher in the high-UPF group. 📊 Chart 3 Interpretation — The Adolescent Peak

UPF peaks sharply at age 6–18 (over 50%). Young children (2–5) have lower intake — parents control their food. As children gain independence (6–12), UPF spikes — this maps perfectly onto peer group socialisation, not genetics. Adolescents (13–18) maintain elevated intake. Adults regain dietary control and UPF falls. The 55+ group was the only age group increasing between 2004 and 2015. The IDU insight: this age-based pattern is driven by social life stage, not biology — it's the clearest chart evidence for nurture shaping dietary identity.

🌍 I&S Perspective
  • Food systems and inequality: extended research shows UPF highest in food-insecure, lower-income households — diet is not just personal choice but a structural social justice issue
  • Peer identity formation: the adolescent UPF spike reflects eating as a social identity signal — certain brands and foods mark group membership
  • Health inequality: Chart 2 outcomes are distributed along socioeconomic lines — a structural inequality, not individual failure
! Limitations
  • Cross-sectional — one snapshot in time, cannot prove causation
  • Self-reported 24-hour dietary recall — people underreport unhealthy foods
  • Canada only — excludes Indigenous communities, military, remote populations
  • 2015 data — landscape has shifted
Reference 06 · Newsweek Article

"I ate ultra-processed foods, my twin didn't. The results were shocking."

Aimee Kingston · Newsweek · 27 June 2023 · Study by Prof. Tim Spector, King's College London
ScienceI&SGC ConnectionJournalism — Not Peer-Reviewed

"Neither of us is overweight or unwell in any way, so I was shocked that as a young, healthy person, it affected my health in as little as two weeks."

— Aimee Kingston, Newsweek, 27 June 2023
Why This is the Most Powerful Source for IDU Integration

Perfect Experimental Design for Nature vs Nurture

Identical twins = 100% shared DNA (controls ALL genetic variables). Matching calories, fat, sugar, fibre (controls macronutrients). Only variable: degree of food processing. Any outcome difference = caused by processing, not genetics, not calories. This is a near-perfect experimental isolation of nurture (diet environment) from nature (genetics).

Aimee — 100% UPF Diet
  • Weight gained in 14 days
  • Blood sugar worsened
  • Blood fat levels worsened
  • Constant hunger despite ↑ calories
  • Mood deteriorated (~1 week)
  • Severe fatigue — visible to others
  • Difficulty concentrating at work
  • Post-study UPF cravings persisted
Nancy — Non-UPF Diet
  • Lost weight in 14 days
  • Blood results improved
  • Blood fat levels improved
  • Rarely felt hungry
  • Often couldn't finish meals
  • Consistently better mood
  • Expanded food repertoire post-study
  • No post-study cravings reported
🔬 Science Concepts Applied
  • Satiety hormone disruption: UPF overrides leptin (fullness) signalling — Aimee hungry despite more calories than usual
  • Metabolic biomarkers: blood sugar and fat worsening in 14 days = rapid metabolic disruption
  • Food dependency: dopamine reward pathway activation — explains post-study cravings despite not enjoying the food
  • Gut microbiome: Spector's ZOE research shows UPF reduces microbial diversity — one mechanism for metabolic harm
→ GC Connections
  • Identical genetics, different diet = measurably different health bodies in 14 days — the purest proof nurture reshapes the biological self
  • Aimee's food identity was unconsciously constructed — diet identity can be shaped by marketing without awareness
  • Post-study identity shift: Aimee now eats "mindfully" — experience transformed her self-concept as a food consumer
→ Ref 5 population data→ Twin methodology
! Limitations
  • N=1 pair — single case, cannot generalise
  • Newsweek = journalism, not peer-reviewed
  • 14 days too short for long-term conclusions
  • No blinding — both knew which diet, creating placebo effects on self-reported mood
Reference 07 · Scientific Classification System

Somatotyping

Heath-Carter method · Endomorph–Mesomorph–Ectomorph (1–7 scale) · Somatoplot diagram
ScienceI&SGC Connection
The Three Body Types

Endomorph · Mesomorph · Ectomorph

Endomorph: wide shoulders AND wide hips, higher body fat → Sumo wrestling (5-5-1)
Mesomorph: wide shoulders, narrow hips, high muscle mass → Wrestling (4-7-1), Weightlifting (2-7-2), Sprinting (1-6-3)
Ectomorph: narrow shoulders and hips, lean → High jump (2-3-5), Volleyball (3-3-6)
Balanced: Swimming (2-4-4), Tennis (2-5-3)

🔬 Science Perspective
  • Body composition is primarily genetically determined — Ref 3 shows weight (0.87) and height (0.92) are highly heritable
  • But somatotype IS modifiable: diet (Refs 5/6) and training alter body composition — nurture acting on a genetic baseline
  • No somatotype guarantees success — Phelps has disproportionate arm length; Kipchoge doesn't fit the "ideal" marathon runner profile
  • Somatotyping is descriptive, not predictive
🌍 I&S Perspective
  • Biological determinism in sport: labelling an athlete's somatotype may constrain their self-exploration — "you're built for wrestling" becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy (Ref 2 link)
  • Cultural bias: sports mapped on the somatoplot are not equally accessible globally — sumo is culturally specific; volleyball infrastructure varies enormously
  • Historical critique: Sheldon's original 1940s system was linked to problematic racial typology — modern use is neutral but the history warrants critical awareness
→ GC Connections
  • Physical body = identity marker that shapes athletic identity, social role, and group membership
  • Somatotype label from a coach = nominative determinism applied to the body (Ref 2)
  • Diet changes somatotype — environment shapes the nature baseline (Refs 5/6)
  • Apply directly to Ref 8: which athlete's somatotype best suits their identified sport?
→ Apply to Ref 8→ Heritability evidence

Applied Analysis: Akira vs Cirrus — The Core Nature/Nurture Comparison

Same life stage. Similar genetic potential. Completely different outcomes.

Both are young males in prime athletic years. Both have high-mesomorph somatotypes suited to power sports. The genetic difference (3-7-2 vs 5-5-1) is real but not enormous — both have athletic potential. Yet: Akira = 1km to facilities, affluent family, name means "brave warrior". Cirrus = 50km barrier, unemployed, knee injury, name means "cloud". This comparison is the living proof of Ref 1's core claim — genes set the starting point, but environment determines whether potential is ever realised.

🌍 I&S Concepts Applied
  • Intersectionality (Crenshaw): Uju faces compounding disadvantages — female, African, married, child, age 24 narrowing athletic window
  • Social mobility through sport: Cirrus's knee injury didn't just end sport — it ended his primary economic strategy, reinforcing pre-existing inequality
  • Role conflict: Uju holds professional, family, and athletic identities simultaneously — all demand incompatible time commitments
  • Nominative determinism applied: Victoria (winner) has everything aligned; Cirrus (cloud) is adrift — does name predict fate, or do social structures explain the pattern?
→ GC — Unit Synthesis
  • This source synthesises the entire unit: names (Ref 2), heritability (Ref 3), twin parallels (Ref 4), diet (Refs 5/6), body type (Ref 7)
  • Victoria = the ultimate question: is her success due to name, genes, or social privilege? All three?
  • Best exam question: "Which athlete is most likely to realise their potential — and is that nature or nurture?"
