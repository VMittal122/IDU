Global context: Identities and relationships — How do genetics, name, diet, body type and social environment work together to shape who we are and how we relate to others?
Reference 1 — Nature and nurture (video)
Type: introductory explainer video / script
Science Individuals & Societies GC connection
Science perspective
  • Nature = genetic inheritance; traits coded in DNA (eye colour, potential height, biological starting point)
  • Nurture = environmental inputs: relationships, schooling, culture, diet, social surroundings
  • The two always interact — genes set a ceiling of potential; environment determines how much of that potential is reached
  • Key scientific concept: phenotype = genotype + environment (gene–environment interaction, GEI)
  • Basketball analogy: height = genetic, but coaching + practice = environmental — elite performance needs both
Individuals & societies perspective
  • Raises the question of agency vs biological determinism — are we free agents or pre-determined?
  • Cultural environment is a socially constructed force: schools, peer groups, media shape identity
  • Parental decisions are social choices with biological outcomes (diet, school, neighbourhood)
GC — identities and relationships
  • This is your theoretical framework for the whole unit — every other source tests either nature or nurture or both
  • Identity is co-produced: it's dynamic and relational, not fixed
  • Relationships (coaches, parents, friends) are environmental inputs that reshape biological potential
  • Use this as your anchor point in any essay: define nature/nurture here, then prove it with other sources
Limitations to note
  • Very simplified — doesn't address epigenetics, gene expression, or gene–environment correlations
  • Not peer-reviewed; designed for young learners
  • No data or citations to back up claims
Reference 2 — Nominative determinism (video)
Type: explainer video / script · Core concept: your name shapes your life path
Individuals & Societies GC connection
Core concept breakdown
  • Nominative determinism: people gravitate towards a life path that matches the meaning of their name
  • Historical names (Baker, Carpenter, Painter) were literally caused by occupation — environment → name
  • Modern question: does the name now cause the path? Direction of causation reverses
  • Multiple athletes named Victoria (Latin: winner/conqueror) all became champions — pattern or coincidence?
  • Winner/Loser brothers: raised in the same family, same environment. "Loser" became a successful police detective; "Winner" ended up in prison. Their lives were the opposite of their names
Individuals & societies perspective
  • Names are cultural identity markers assigned before any personal choice is possible
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy (sociology): believing you are a winner can generate winning behaviour
  • The Winner/Loser paradox shows identity can also resist or subvert expectation — names are not destiny
GC — identities and relationships
  • A name is the very first identity label — assigned by others (parents/culture) before any self-agency
  • Cross-reference with Ref 8: all four athlete profiles have meaningful names (Akira = brave warrior, Victoria = winner, Cirrus = cloud)
  • Cross-reference with Ref 4: twins Divia and Lilly share the surname Baker — connects name meaning to the blog case study
  • Challenges agency: is identity something you build, or something named into you from birth?
  • The Winner/Loser paradox is a great discussion point — shows the limits of environmental/nominal influence
Limitations to note
  • Entirely anecdotal — one sibling pair and a handful of athletes is not statistically valid
  • Confirmation bias: we remember the cases where name matches life and forget the counter-examples
  • No peer-reviewed evidence cited

Trend analysis: MZ correlations are consistently roughly double DZ correlations across every single trait. This "≈2× rule" is the statistical fingerprint of genetic heritability — if traits were purely environmental, MZ and DZ correlations would be equal (both raised together). Physical traits (height 0.92, weight 0.87) show the strongest genetic signal. Internal functions (heart 0.55) show weaker but still significant genetic influence. Even higher cognitive function scores 0.69 MZ vs 0.38 DZ — thinking patterns and intelligence have a strong genetic component. The overall study finding: average heritability across all 17,804 human traits is approximately 49%.
Science perspective
  • MZ twins share 100% DNA; DZ twins share ~50% (like any siblings) but both share the same household environment
  • The MZ–DZ gap isolates genetic influence: the bigger the gap, the more heritable the trait
  • Meta-analysis of 2,748 studies = extremely high statistical power; most comprehensive twin data ever compiled
  • Published in Nature Genetics (top-tier, peer-reviewed) — your highest credibility source in the whole IDU
  • Key finding from the extended paper: 69% of traits show patterns consistent with purely additive genetic variation — shared environment adds little
  • Heritability tends to decrease with age for most traits
GC — identities and relationships
  • Quantifies exactly how much of "who we are" is genetically determined — the best scientific evidence for the nature side of Ref 1
  • Even higher cognitive function (thought, personality, decision-making) is ~69% correlated in identical twins — our inner identity has a biological core
  • But no trait reaches 1.0 — identical twins always differ somewhat. Environment always plays a role. Identity is never purely genetic
  • Use the numbers in essays: "Ref 3 shows height is 0.92 correlated in MZ twins vs 0.47 in DZ twins, confirming a strong genetic basis for physical identity"
  • Cross-reference with Ref 4 twins: Divia/Lilly and Fred/Callum are the real-life human stories behind these statistics
Limitations to note
  • Correlation ≠ causation. High MZ correlation shows genetic influence, not a specific gene causing the trait
  • "Equal environments assumption" (EEA): the model assumes MZ and DZ twins experience equally similar environments — this has been questioned
  • Study pool skewed toward European, North American and Australian participants — limited global representation
Full paper: nature.com/articles/ng.3285 (Polderman et al., Nature Genetics, 2015)
Reference 4 — Twins separated at birth (blog)
Type: anecdotal blog case studies · Two pairs: Divia & Lilly / Fred & Callum
Science Individuals & Societies GC connection
Case 1: Divia (India) and Lilly (South Africa)
  • Separated at birth; different continents, different cultures; reunited after 34 years
  • Similarities: same hairstyle, identical laugh, same lipstick shade, same degree (Anthropology), same career (museum worker), same hobby (baking), same nail-biting habit, identical leg-crossing posture
  • Share the surname Baker — Ref 2 connection: nominative determinism
  • Key difference: music taste (Divia = rock, Lilly = classical) — a culturally driven divergence
  • Name meanings differ: Divia = divinely brilliant; Lilly = flower — yet their lives converged remarkably
Case 2: Fred and Callum (both meaning: peace)
  • Same height, weight, brown curly hair
  • Both married a woman named Jane; both divorced after exactly 12 years
  • Both love river fishing; both have won fishing contests
  • Both have the same unusual hammering headache type
  • Both named their dog Elvis; both dislike cats
  • Both in law enforcement (different fields) — slight environmental divergence in career path
Science perspective
  • Separated twin design is the gold standard for separating nature from nurture — no shared home environment
  • Any similarity when raised apart = strong genetic signal
  • Cases qualitatively support Ref 3: physical traits (height, weight, hair) AND behavioural traits (habits, hobbies, relationship patterns) both show strong MZ convergence even without shared environment
  • Music difference (Divia/Lilly) directly illustrates the role of cultural environment (nurture) even with identical genetics
  • Fred/Callum marrying women named Jane = fascinating, but could be coincidence; not peer-reviewed
GC — identities and relationships
  • If two people raised in entirely different countries/cultures end up nearly identical — what does individual identity mean?
  • Baker surname connection to Ref 2 is a strong essay link: did their name predict their shared love of baking?
  • The relationship patterns (both married Jane, both divorced) suggest genetics may influence who we are attracted to and how we bond
  • Music difference = the power of cultural environment to shape preference even when biology is identical
Limitations to note
  • Only two cases — not statistically representative
  • Blog format = not peer-reviewed; written for narrative impact, may exaggerate similarities
  • Some similarities may be coincidence (e.g. both calling dog "Elvis")

Science perspective
  • NOVA classification system (4 groups) is a widely accepted scientific framework for food processing
  • Large sample sizes (13,608 and 20,080) give high statistical power
  • Dose–response in Chart 2 strengthens the biological argument that UPF causes harm
  • Adolescent UPF spike (Chart 3) has a biological explanation: developing brains are more susceptible to reward-driven eating, and UPF exploits dopamine pathways
GC — identities and relationships
  • Diet = environmental nurture factor that biologically alters the body — directly connects to Ref 1 framework
  • Adolescent UPF spike shows how peer culture and social norms shape eating identity during identity formation years
  • Extended research shows UPF consumption highest in food-insecure households — identity shaped by socioeconomic inequality
  • Cross-reference with Ref 6 (Aimee Kingston): the population-level data here becomes lived individual experience there — use both together in essays
Limitation: Cross-sectional study = single snapshot in time, cannot prove causation. Self-reported 24-hour dietary recall is prone to underreporting. Conducted only in Canada's 10 provinces — excludes Indigenous communities, military, remote populations. May not generalise globally.

Reference 6 — Newsweek UPF twin article (Aimee Kingston)
Published: 27 June 2023 · Newsweek · Study by Prof. Tim Spector, King's College London / TwinsUK registry
Science Individuals & Societies GC connection
Study design — why it's powerful
  • Identical twins share 100% DNA — the perfect genetic control group
  • Aimee: 100% UPF for 2 weeks
  • Nancy (twin): same calories, fat, sugar, fibre — but entirely non/low-processed foods
  • Any difference in outcomes is caused by food processing, not genetics, not calorie count
  • This isolates nurture (diet environment) from nature (genetics) perfectly
Aimee's UPF results
  • Weight gain; worsened blood sugar levels; worsened blood fat levels
  • Constant hunger despite eating more calories than her usual pre-study diet
  • Mood deterioration within about one week
  • Severe fatigue — visible to friends and family by end of two weeks
  • Difficulty concentrating at work in afternoons — cognitive impairment
  • Post-study: body still craved UPF — signs of behavioural dependency forming
Nancy's non-UPF results
  • Lost weight; improved blood results
  • Rarely hungry; often couldn't finish meals — healthy satiety signals working
  • Consistently better mood throughout
  • Tried new foods post-study (quinoa porridge) — expanded her food identity
Science perspective
  • UPF disrupts satiety hormone signalling (leptin/ghrelin pathway) — explains hunger despite adequate calories
  • Blood marker changes in only 2 weeks show rapid metabolic impact — supports and personalises the population data from Ref 5
  • Twin design eliminates genetic confounding — among the cleanest possible natural experiments
  • Prof. Spector's ZOE research also shows gut microbiome responses to UPF vary between individuals due to their unique genetic and microbial make-up
  • Very sweet refined carbohydrates caused worst mood/energy crashes — possible blood glucose spiking
GC — identities and relationships
  • Diet directly shapes how you feel, think, and relate to others — it's not just physical but profoundly relational and identity-altering
  • Aimee didn't realise how much UPF she was already eating — food identity can be unconscious and socially constructed
  • Convenience, social eating environments and marketing are societal forces that shape diet identity
  • Core GC argument: Aimee and Nancy have identical genetics, but 14 days of different environmental input (diet) produced measurably different health identities
  • Post-study behaviour change = identity shift: experience reshaped Aimee's relationship with food and her self-concept as a "healthy eater"
Limitations to note
  • N=1 pair — a single case study, not a population sample; cannot generalise
  • Published in Newsweek (journalism, not a peer-reviewed journal); narrative framing may amplify effects
  • Two-week period is very short for drawing long-term health conclusions
  • Meals were prepared by a chef — not reflective of typical UPF consumption patterns
Reference 7 — Somatotyping (classification diagram)
Type: scientific classification system · Format: somatoplot diagram showing body types and associated sports
Science Individuals & Societies GC connection
What somatotyping is
  • Three-dimensional body composition system: Endomorph (fat/round) — Mesomorph (muscular) — Ectomorph (lean/linear)
  • Scale: 1–7 for each component. Format: Endo-Meso-Ecto. Example: 4-5-2 = moderate fat, dominant muscle, low lean = Football
  • Mesomorph (centre top): wide shoulders, narrow hips — power sports (wrestling 4-7-1, weightlifting 2-7-2)
  • Ectomorph (right): narrow shoulders and hips — height/endurance sports (high jump 2-3-5, volleyball 3-3-6)
  • Endomorph (left): wide shoulders AND wide hips — sumo wrestling (5-5-1)
  • Swimming (2-4-4) = balanced mesomorph–ectomorph; sprinting (1-6-3) = dominant mesomorph with ectomorph lean
Science perspective
  • Body type is primarily genetically determined (Ref 3: height 0.92, weight maintenance 0.87 MZ correlations)
  • But somatotype is modifiable: diet (Refs 5/6) and training alter body composition — nurture acting on a genetic base
  • Used by sports scientists and coaches to match athletes to disciplines and design tailored training programmes
  • No somatotype is a guarantee of success — many elite athletes don't perfectly match the "ideal" somatotype for their sport (e.g. Michael Phelps has unusually long arms for his height)
GC — identities and relationships
  • Physical body shape is an identity marker that shapes athletic identity — and by extension social role, group membership, and self-perception
  • Being labelled a certain somatotype by a coach can become a self-fulfilling prophecy (directly mirrors Ref 2 nominative determinism)
  • Cross-reference with Ref 8: apply the somatoplot to each athlete profile, then ask whether environment (access, finances, injury, age) allows or prevents them from pursuing their genetic potential
  • Biological determinism question: is your athletic identity written in your body shape? Or can nurture overcome a non-ideal somatotype?
  • Diet (Refs 5/6) can shift somatotype — environment changes the nature baseline
Limitations to note
  • Somatotyping is descriptive, not predictive — correlates body type with sport, but does not determine success
  • Cultural and historical bias in sport selection: which sports are played where varies by country, access, and culture
  • Original system (Sheldon, 1940s) had problematic racial assumptions — modern use is more neutral but worth noting

Science — applying the somatoplot (Ref 7)
  • Akira (3-7-2): near-identical to wrestling (4-7-1) and weightlifting (2-7-2) profiles. Strong genetic match. Environmental conditions fully support pursuit
  • Uju (2-4-4): exact match to swimming on the somatoplot. Body genetics align, but location (4 km) and life stage (married, child, career) create competing demands
  • Cirrus (5-5-1): sumo/football profile. The genetics are there — but 50 km to facilities, unemployment, and a knee injury create an almost insurmountable environmental barrier. Name = cloud = his dreams remain out of reach
  • Victoria (1-6-2): near-match for sprinting (1-6-3). Close to perfect alignment of genetics, name, access and structural support (scholarship)
Individuals & societies perspective
  • Access to sports facilities is unequally distributed — social inequality constrains biological potential
  • Akira vs Cirrus: same life stage, similar athletic genetic potential (both high mesomorph) — but completely different socioeconomic environments. The clearest nature vs nurture comparison in the unit
  • Gender intersections: Victoria (N. America, scholarship) has institutional support that Uju (Africa, family, career at 24) does not
  • Age matters: at 24 with a family and career, Uju's athletic window may be narrowing — social life stage constrains biological potential
GC — identities and relationships
  • Victoria is the strongest nominative determinism case in the entire IDU: name means winner, body type is ideal, scholarship provides institutional identity — ask "does she succeed because of her name, her genes, or her environment?"
  • Cirrus = cloud: his athletic aspiration is literally "floating" — name metaphor aligns with his situation (inaccessible, unreachable)
