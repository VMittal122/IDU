Every I&S concept used across all 8 sources — with the thinker who coined it, its definition, IDU application, and links to the science perspective.

Identity Theory
Refs 1, 2, 4, 6 · How identity is formed and constructed
Concept 01

Nominative Determinism

Ref 2New Scientist, 1994

The hypothesis that people tend to gravitate toward careers and life paths that match the meaning of their name. Originally an observation in psychology journals — researchers named "Mind" publishing neuroscience, or "Gold" publishing financial research.

Not a scientific law — weak and disputed evidence. But sociologically interesting because it raises the question of whether the social expectations attached to a name shape behaviour over time.

IDU Application

Ref 2: Multiple athletes named Victoria (= winner/conqueror) became champions. Ref 4: Both Baker twins love baking. Ref 8: Victoria (winner) has every advantage; Cirrus (cloud) is adrift; Akira (brave warrior) has the resources to act on his warrior genetics.

Science Bridge

Neuroplasticity explains the mechanism: repeated social expectations shaped by a name can rewire neural pathways for confidence and aspiration over time — giving a biological pathway for a sociological phenomenon.

Concept 02

Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

Refs 2, 7, 8Merton, 1948

A belief or prediction that, by being widely held, causes itself to become true through the actions it inspires. Robert Merton coined the term in 1948.

Mechanism: label is applied → person internalises it → their behaviour changes to match the label → the prediction comes true → the label is "confirmed."

This can be positive (a child told they're gifted works harder and excels) or negative (a child told they'll fail becomes disengaged).

IDU Application

Victoria Johnson (Ref 8): name = "winner" → coaches invest more → scholarship obtained → access to elite training → success. Each step is a self-fulfilling cycle. Cirrus: name = "cloud" + perceived failure (lost contract) → potentially internalised as identity → reduced motivation to rebuild.

Concept 03

Labelling Theory

Refs 2, 8Becker, 1963

From Howard Becker's sociology: once a label is assigned to a person, it shapes how others treat them AND how they see themselves. Labels become self-defining.

Becker was writing about deviance (how labelling someone a "criminal" shapes their identity and behaviour) but the concept applies to any identity label — including names.

Key insight: the label doesn't describe a pre-existing reality — it creates one. "Winner" and "Loser" as names created social realities through how others responded to each brother.

IDU Application — Winner/Loser Paradox

"Loser" Lane became a detective partly because the label triggered resistance (identity defiance). "Winner" Lane's criminal path may reflect the perverse pressure of an impossible name. The label influenced both outcomes — just not in the expected direction. This is Labelling Theory operating in two opposing directions simultaneously.

Concept 04

Social Construction of Identity

Refs 1, 2, 6Berger & Luckmann, 1966

The sociological position that identity is not biologically fixed or naturally given, but is constructed through social interactions, cultural practices, historical context, and language.

Contrasts with essentialism (identity has a fixed biological core — supported by Ref 3's heritability data) and sits at the heart of the nature/nurture debate.

Key evidence in the IDU: Aimee Kingston (Ref 6) thought she had a "healthy eater" identity — but post-study discovered this identity was socially constructed by food marketing, not by actual dietary behaviour.

Science Bridge

Ref 3's heritability data challenges pure social constructivism: if cognitive function and physical traits are ~50-90% heritable, then biology IS part of identity's foundation. The IDU synthesis: identity is co-constructed by both biological nature and social environment.

Social Structures & Inequality
Refs 5, 8 · How society constrains and enables identity
Concept 05

Intersectionality

Ref 8Crenshaw, 1989

The concept that multiple social identities (gender, race, class, ability, geography, age) do not operate independently — they interact and compound to create unique experiences of privilege or disadvantage.

Kimberlé Crenshaw coined the term in the context of racial and gender discrimination. The key insight: you cannot fully understand someone's social position by looking at one identity dimension at a time.

IDU Application — Uju Ogbonna

Uju faces compounding disadvantages: female in sport (structural gender inequality) + African (geographic sport infrastructure deficit) + married with a child (family role demands) + age 24 (narrowing athletic window). No single factor alone explains her constrained athletic potential — their intersection creates a unique compound disadvantage that Akira or Victoria simply do not face.

Concept 06

Cultural Capital

Refs 8, 2Bourdieu, 1986

Pierre Bourdieu's concept of non-financial social assets that promote social mobility. Three forms:
Embodied: skills, knowledge, ways of speaking
Institutionalised: academic qualifications, certifications, scholarships
Objectified: books, equipment, access to facilities

Cultural capital explains why some individuals have structural advantages beyond just money — and why these advantages compound over time.

IDU Application

Victoria Johnson's sports scholarship = institutionalised cultural capital. Her 0.5km proximity to facilities = objectified capital. Her name meaning "winner" may confer subtle embodied capital (confidence, expected treatment from coaches). Cirrus lacks all three forms — his genetic potential (nature) is blocked by a cultural capital deficit (nurture).

Concept 07

Social Mobility Through Sport

Ref 8

Sport can function as a vehicle for upward social mobility — providing income, status, and networks to individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds. It is one of the few meritocratic pathways that can transcend class barriers.

However, sporting social mobility is structurally precarious: a single injury, loss of form, or contract failure can eliminate the pathway entirely — often leaving the athlete without educational qualifications or other income sources.

IDU Application — Cirrus Rodriguez

His professional rugby contract would have transformed his economic position. The knee injury didn't just end sport — it ended his primary economic strategy, likely reinforcing the pre-existing social inequality. The structural fragility of sport-based mobility is the IDU's clearest I&S critique of the "sport is meritocratic" narrative.

Concept 08

Role Conflict

Ref 8Merton, 1957

Occurs when a person occupies multiple social roles (parent, professional, athlete) that demand incompatible behaviours or time commitments.

Unlike role strain (difficulty meeting the demands of one role), role conflict arises between different roles — the obligations of being a solicitor, wife, and mother directly conflict with the time demands of being an elite athlete.

IDU Application — Uju Ogbonna

Uju simultaneously holds three competing roles: solicitor (9–5 work demands), wife and mother (family time), and aspiring athlete (training time). All three compete for the same finite daily hours. The I&S analysis reveals that what appears to be a personal choice not to pursue sport is actually a structural role conflict — Uju is constrained by social role obligations that athletes like Victoria (18, single, scholarship-supported) simply don't have.

Socialisation & Identity Formation
Refs 1, 2, 5 · How social processes shape who we become
Concept 09

Socialisation

Ref 1Durkheim

The lifelong process by which individuals learn the norms, values, beliefs, and behaviours of their society.

Primary socialisation: occurs in early childhood through the family — the most powerful agent of socialisation. Shapes language, values, habits, and basic identity.
Secondary socialisation: through peers, school, media, religion, and work — expands and sometimes challenges primary socialisation. More relevant during adolescence and adulthood.

IDU Application

The adolescent UPF spike in Ref 5's Chart 3 is driven by secondary socialisation — peer groups, school canteens, and social eating norms replace parental food control. This is the I&S explanation for why UPF consumption peaks at ages 6–18 and then falls in adulthood — it tracks the secondary socialisation life stage.

Concept 10

False Consciousness & Food Marketing

Ref 6Marx-adjacent concept

False consciousness: when people are unaware of how social/economic systems are influencing their beliefs and behaviours. Applied to food: food companies market UPF as "whole grain," "natural," "high fibre," or "artisan" — constructing a false "healthy eater" identity in consumers actually buying industrially processed products.

Aimee Kingston's central revelation in Ref 6: she believed her diet was balanced before the study, but she was actually consuming far more UPF than she realised — her healthy-eater identity was a marketing construction, not a nutritional reality.

Science Bridge

The biological mechanism reinforces this: UPF are engineered to be hyperpalatable without triggering satiety signals — the body doesn't register fullness (Science), which is why people keep eating (I&S: mindless consumption) without conscious awareness of overconsumption.

Concept 11

Essentialism vs Constructivism

Refs 3, 4, 6

Essentialism: identity has a fixed, biological core that exists prior to social experience. The "essential self" is natural and pre-social. Supported by Ref 3's heritability data and Ref 4's separated twins.

Social Constructivism: identity is entirely built through social experience — there is no pre-social essential self. Supported by Ref 6's dietary experiment (identical genetics, divergent health identities) and Ref 5's social patterns of diet.

The IDU synthesis: neither extreme is correct. Ref 3 shows biology is real (~49% heritability). Ref 6 shows environment is real (identical twins diverge in 14 days). Identity is the ongoing interaction of both.

Concept 12

Peer Group Influence & Adolescent Identity

Ref 5 Chart 3

During adolescence, peer groups become the dominant socialisation agent, displacing family influence. Adolescents construct identity through group membership — adopting the behaviours, tastes, and consumption patterns of their peer group as identity signals.

What you eat, what music you listen to, what brands you wear — all become identity markers in adolescence. UPF brands deliberately exploit this developmental psychology, marketing their products as youth identity markers.

IDU Application

Chart 3 (Ref 5) shows UPF consumption jumping sharply from young children (2–5) to children (6–12) as parental control gives way to peer influence. This age-based pattern is not genetic — it maps perfectly onto the sociology of adolescent identity formation.

The Three IDU Perspectives on Identity — A Summary

🧬 Biological / Given

Ref 3: ~49% heritability. Ref 4: separated twins converge on habits, careers, and relationships despite different environments. Identity has a heritable biological core that precedes social experience.

🌍 Social / Assigned

Ref 2: names are socially assigned before any choice. Ref 8: socioeconomic access determines which genetic potential gets realised. Ref 5: social class and food systems determine diet identity. Society assigns and constrains identity.

⚡ Co-produced / Dynamic

Ref 6: identical genetics + different diet = different health identity in 14 days. Ref 1's GEI framework. Identity is neither purely given nor purely assigned — it is continuously co-produced by biological and social forces in interaction.
