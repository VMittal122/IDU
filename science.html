Genetics & Heredity
Refs 1, 3, 4 · The biological foundation of identity
Concept 01

Genotype vs Phenotype

Refs 1, 3, 4
Phenotype = Genotype + Environment

Genotype is your complete DNA sequence — fixed at conception, inherited from both parents. It contains every gene that codes for proteins, traits, and biological tendencies.

Phenotype is everything observable: height, weight, eye colour, metabolism, behaviour, disease susceptibility. Phenotype is not just your genes — it is the result of genes interacting with every environmental input from womb onwards.

IDU Application

Divia and Lilly (Ref 4) have identical genotypes but diverge in music taste — a phenotypic difference caused by different cultural environments. Their shared behavioural habits (nail-biting, leg-crossing) are genotype expressing consistently despite different environments.

Concept 02

Heritability (h²)

Ref 3
h² = 2(rMZ − rDZ)

Heritability is the proportion of observed variation in a trait between people in a population that is attributable to genetic differences. Scale: 0.0 (entirely environmental) to 1.0 (entirely genetic).

Critical misconception: h²=0.92 for height does NOT mean "92% of your height is determined by genes." It means "in this population, 92% of the differences in height between people can be explained by genetic variation." In a population where everyone has identical diets, heritability would be higher. In a famine, it would be lower.

IDU Numbers to Quote

Height: 0.92 MZ vs 0.47 DZ. Weight maintenance: 0.87 MZ vs 0.42 DZ. Higher cognitive function: 0.69 MZ vs 0.38 DZ. Average across all 17,804 traits: ~49%.

Concept 03

MZ vs DZ Twin Studies

Refs 3, 4, 6

Monozygotic (MZ) twins: one fertilised egg splits into two embryos. Share 100% DNA. Any difference between MZ twins must be environmental.

Dizygotic (DZ) twins: two separately fertilised eggs. Share ~50% DNA (same as any siblings). Both typically share the same home environment as MZ twins.

The MZ–DZ comparison is powerful because: same home environment for both, but MZ have twice the genetic overlap. The gap between MZ and DZ correlations isolates genetic effects from shared environment.

IDU Link

Ref 3's entire dataset uses this logic. Ref 4's separated twins strengthen it further — no shared environment at all. Ref 6's experiment controls it perfectly: 100% shared DNA, only diet differs.

Concept 04

Meta-Analysis

Ref 3

A statistical technique that combines data from multiple independent studies to produce one more powerful conclusion. Polderman et al. (2015) pooled 2,748 studies and 14.5 million twin pairs — far greater statistical certainty than any single study could achieve.

Meta-analysis reduces the risk that one unusual study skews the conclusion. It also allows comparison across different populations and time periods (1958–2012 in Ref 3).

Why This Matters for Credibility

Ref 3 is your highest credibility source precisely because it IS a meta-analysis. When writing about nature/nurture, "a 2015 meta-analysis of 2,748 studies" is far stronger than citing a single twin pair (Ref 4) or a journalist's account (Ref 6).

Concept 05

Gene-Environment Interaction (GEI)

Refs 1, 3, 4

The phenomenon where the effect of a gene depends on the environment, and the effect of an environment depends on the genotype. Neither operates independently.

Example: a gene linked to height potential only produces tall stature if nutrition is adequate during childhood. The gene's effect is modified by the environmental input. Conversely, the effect of malnutrition on height depends on the individual's genetic potential.

IDU Application (Ref 1 Basketball)

Genetic height = the nature component. Coaching, practice, and strategy = the nurture component. The athletic outcome requires both — elite basketball performance is the GEI product, not height alone nor training alone.

IDU Link

This concept is the scientific translation of the core IDU argument: genes and environment co-produce outcomes. Victoria's athletic success = her 1-6-2 somatotype (GEI) interacting with scholarship access and training environment.

Concept 06

Epigenetics

Advanced — not directly in sources

Mechanisms by which environmental factors switch genes "on" or "off" without changing the DNA sequence. Chemical tags (methyl groups) added to DNA or histones alter whether genes are expressed. Diet, stress, trauma, and toxic exposure can all trigger epigenetic changes.

Why it matters for this IDU: epigenetics is the molecular mechanism by which nurture modifies nature. It blurs the nature/nurture boundary — the environment literally writes on your DNA (without changing the sequence). Aimee Kingston's rapid metabolic changes in Ref 6 may partially involve epigenetic mechanisms.

IDU Link

Use in essays to show Ref 1's framework is an oversimplification: it's not just that environment shapes how genes express — it can epigenetically alter gene expression itself. Nature is not as fixed as Ref 1 implies.

Nutrition Science
Refs 5, 6 · Diet as an environmental identity-shaper
Concept 07

NOVA Food Classification

Ref 5

A system classifying foods by the nature, extent, and purpose of industrial processing — not by nutrients. Four groups:

Group 1: Unprocessed/minimally processed (fresh vegetables, plain meat, milk)
Group 2: Processed culinary ingredients (sugar, salt, oil, butter)
Group 3: Processed foods (canned vegetables, artisan bread, cheeses)
Group 4: Ultra-processed foods (UPF) — industrial formulations with additives, emulsifiers, artificial flavours: chips, mass-produced cereal, instant noodles

Key Insight

NOVA does NOT classify by nutrients. A food can be nutritionally similar to an unprocessed equivalent but still be Group 4 if it uses industrial additives. This is why fruity yogurts, breakfast cereals, and "whole grain" sliced bread can be UPF — not because they're nutritionally empty, but because they're industrially formulated.

Concept 08

Dose-Response Relationship

Ref 5 Chart 2

A fundamental epidemiological concept: as the dose (exposure) increases, so does the response (effect). In Ref 5's Chart 2, every health condition (obesity, diabetes, hypertension, heart disease) worsens consistently from low → medium → high UPF groups.

A dose-response gradient is stronger evidence of causation than a simple binary comparison because it shows a consistent pattern across the full exposure range — making it less likely to be explained by chance or a single confounding variable.

Limitation to Note

Dose-response strengthens the case but does not prove causation alone — the study is still cross-sectional. Ref 6's experimental design is required to establish the causal direction.

Concept 09

Satiety Hormone Disruption

Ref 6

Leptin: the "fullness" hormone, secreted by fat cells to signal satiety to the brain. High protein and fibre intake strongly triggers leptin release.

Ghrelin: the "hunger" hormone, produced in the stomach — signals hunger to the brain. UPFs are low in fibre and protein, leaving ghrelin elevated even after eating.

UPFs are engineered to be hyperpalatable and energy-dense while bypassing satiety signals. This explains why Aimee Kingston (Ref 6) was "hungry all the time despite eating more calories than usual" — her hormonal feedback system was circumvented by UPF's food engineering.

IDU Application

This is the biological mechanism behind Chart 2's obesity association (Ref 5). It also explains why Ref 6's results changed in just 14 days — hormonal disruption is rapid.

Concept 10

Cross-Sectional vs Experimental Design

Refs 5, 6

Cross-sectional study (Ref 5): collects data from a population at one point in time. Can identify associations but cannot establish causation. Ref 5 shows UPF intake correlates with worse health — but sick people may eat more UPF (reverse causation), or low income causes both (confounding).

Controlled experiment (Ref 6): manipulates one variable (diet type) while controlling all others (genetics via twin design, calories, macronutrients). Any outcome difference must be caused by the manipulated variable. Establishes causation.

Why Use Both Together

Ref 5's large sample (13,608 adults) gives statistical power and real-world generalisability. Ref 6's experimental design provides causal proof. Together they form a complete argument: the association exists at population scale (Ref 5) and has a causal mechanism (Ref 6).

Concept 11

Metabolic Biomarkers

Ref 6

Measurable substances in the blood indicating metabolic health status. Key biomarkers in Ref 6:

Blood glucose: indicates how well the body regulates blood sugar. Elevated = insulin resistance risk.
Triglycerides/cholesterol: blood fat levels associated with cardiovascular risk.

Aimee's blood glucose and fat levels worsened in just 14 days. This rapid change confirms that metabolic disruption from UPF is fast-acting — not a gradual years-long process. It supports Ref 5's associations with diabetes and heart disease.

Concept 12

Gut Microbiome

Ref 6 — Spector/ZOE research

The gut microbiome is the community of trillions of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses) living in the digestive system. It plays a critical role in immune function, metabolism, mood regulation, and nutrient absorption.

Prof. Spector's ZOE research (referenced in Ref 6's context) shows UPFs tend to reduce microbiome diversity and beneficial bacteria populations. This is one proposed mechanism for why UPF causes the health outcomes seen in Ref 5 — reduced microbiome diversity may impair metabolic signalling, immune function, and inflammation regulation.

IDU Interest

The microbiome is partially inherited (genetically) but heavily shaped by diet (nurture). This is another example of nature and nurture intersecting — your genetic microbiome baseline is then modified by every meal you eat.

Body Composition & Sport Science
Refs 7, 8 · Physical identity and athletic potential
Concept 13

Somatotyping (Heath-Carter)

Refs 7, 8
Rating: Endomorphy — Mesomorphy — Ectomorphy (1–7 each)

A three-dimensional body composition classification system. Each component rated 1–7:

Endomorphy: relative fatness/roundness — wide hips, tendency to store fat
Mesomorphy: relative musculoskeletal robustness — muscle mass, bone density, wide shoulders
Ectomorphy: relative linearity and leanness — long limbs, narrow build, low fat

A somatotype like 3-7-2 = moderate fat, dominant muscle, low lean. This maps onto the somatoplot to suggest wrestling and weightlifting as ideal sports.

Apply to Ref 8

Akira 3-7-2 → Wrestling/Weightlifting. Uju 2-4-4 → Swimming. Cirrus 5-5-1 → Sumo/Football. Victoria 1-6-2 → Sprinting. The somatotype analysis is the scientific contribution to Ref 8 — the I&S contribution is asking why only some athletes get to pursue their ideal sport.

Concept 14

Body Composition vs BMI

Ref 7

BMI (Body Mass Index = weight ÷ height²) is a crude population health indicator. It does not distinguish between fat mass and muscle mass — a highly muscular mesomorph and an overweight individual can have the same BMI.

Somatotyping captures the structure of the body in three dimensions. A rugby player (high mesomorphy) and a distance runner (high ectomorphy) may have similar BMIs but radically different somatotypes and athletic capabilities.

This is why somatotyping is more useful than BMI for athletic assessment — it reveals the body's actual composition, not just its total mass relative to height.

Concept 15

Neuroplasticity & Behaviour

Ref 2 — Teacher's Science Lens

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganise itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. It is the biological mechanism by which experience and environment alter the brain itself.

This is the teacher's key science question for Ref 2: "How could repeated labels affect a person's behaviour over time?" The answer is neuroplasticity — repeated social expectations (like a name that means "winner") can reshape neural pathways for confidence, risk-taking, and aspiration over years of development.

IDU Bridge Concept

Neuroplasticity bridges the gap between Ref 2 (I&S: name labels shape identity) and Science: the mechanism is biological — repeated social inputs literally rewire the brain. This is how nurture (social labels) modifies nature (brain structure) at a cellular level.

Concept 16

Dopamine Reward Pathway & Food

Ref 6

The mesolimbic dopamine system is the brain's reward pathway. Highly palatable foods (high fat + high sugar combinations, characteristic of UPF) trigger dopamine release — the same pathway activated by addictive substances.

Repeated activation desensitises dopamine receptors — requiring more stimulation to produce the same pleasure signal. This creates habitual seeking behaviour. Aimee Kingston noted she "didn't really enjoy the food but finished everything I was given and was always left wanting more" — a description consistent with dopamine desensitisation and habitual reward-seeking.

IDU Link

Post-study UPF cravings = neurological dependency formation in 14 days. This adds a biological dimension to why UPF consumption is so persistent — it's not just social convenience but neurochemical conditioning.
